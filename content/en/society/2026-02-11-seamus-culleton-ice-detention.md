## When "Legal Status" Doesn't Mean "Protected Status"

The backdrop matters here. Between January 2025 and January 2026, ICE expanded detention operations by 23%. Average detention stays jumped from 37 days to 56 days across all cases. This wasn't targeted enforcement of specific violations—it was a broader net that started catching people who thought they were safely on the right side of immigration law.

Culleton walked into a routine check-in at the New York ICE field office in September 2025. These check-ins are standard for anyone with pending immigration cases, including people transitioning from temporary work visas to green cards. He had his EAD cards—those government-issued documents that literally exist to prove you're authorized to work in the United States.

They detained him anyway. Then they transferred him to Pine Prairie ICE Processing Center in Louisiana.

Look, this isn't supposed to happen. USCIS (U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services) issues work permits. ICE (Immigration and Customs Enforcement) enforces immigration law. Same parent agency—the Department of Homeland Security. You'd think the people approving your work authorization would talk to the people who might detain you.

They don't. Not effectively.

ICE agents use a database called ENFORCE to verify immigration status during encounters. It runs separately from USCIS's systems that track your applications and issue your documents. According to a 2025 Government Accountability Office report, ICE agents frequently make decisions based on information that's 30-90 days out of date. They miss recent approvals, pending applications, updated authorizations.

In Culleton's case, his attorney presented valid EAD cards and approval notices. Didn't matter. ICE's system showed him as deportable based on outdated information, and that designation overrode the physical evidence of his legal status.

Here's the timeline of what five months in detention actually looks like:

**September 2025**: Detained at routine check-in despite valid EAD  
**October-November 2025**: Multiple bond hearings denied; transferred between facilities  
**December 2025**: Irish consular officials get involved; media picks up the story  
**February 2026**: Finally released after five months

Five months. For paperwork verification.

The technical reason involves legacy systems. ICE's ENFORCE database was built in the early 2000s. USCIS deployed modernized case management systems in 2018-2020. They don't integrate well. Data transfers happen through batch uploads instead of real-time connections, creating gaps that persist for weeks or months.

But here's what that means in human terms: You can hold a valid document in your hand, issued by a federal agency, that proves you're legally authorized to be in the country and work. And another federal agency can still lock you up because their computer doesn't show it.

Sound familiar? It should, because this exact vulnerability affects hundreds of thousands of tech workers right now.

## The Documentation Paradox: When Your Papers Don't Protect You

Employment Authorization Documents aren't ambiguous paperwork. They're issued specifically by USCIS to prove lawful work eligibility. They're recognized across federal agencies. In theory.

According to immigration attorneys who spoke with The Guardian about Culleton's case, the problem is how ICE's systems categorize people. USCIS saw Culleton as a legal worker with pending permanent residency. ICE's ENFORCE Alien Removal Module flagged him as someone triggering mandatory detention—possibly a prior removal order or priority enforcement designation that overrode his current authorization.

Immigration law experts call this the "documentation paradox"—you have valid documents, but enforcement systems don't recognize them when it counts.

For tech workers, this gets especially messy because you often hold multiple forms of authorization simultaneously. You might have:
- An H-1B visa
- An approved I-140 immigrant petition (the first step toward a green card)
- An EAD based on pending adjustment of status

If ICE's system only registers your H-1B expiration date and doesn't see the EAD approval, you're suddenly detainable despite full legal compliance.

Now, you might be thinking: "But wouldn't they just check when you show them the documents?"

That's the logical assumption. It's not how this works.

Detention decisions trigger a specific bureaucratic process. Once you're categorized as detainable in the system, getting recategorized requires multiple verification steps, often involving immigration court hearings, manual USCIS record reviews, and coordination between agencies that don't have standardized communication protocols.

Culleton's attorney had the right documents from day one. It still took five months.

## Inside ICE Detention: What Actually Happens

After his September arrest in New York, Culleton was transferred to Louisiana's Pine Prairie ICE Processing Center—1,300 miles from his attorney, his job, his support network.

The Department of Homeland Security Office of Inspector General's 2025 report found that 47% of ICE detention facilities failed to meet basic standards for legal access, medical care, or communication systems. For someone like Culleton, this meant his attorney had to file multiple motions for telephonic hearings, arrange video appearances, navigate facility transfer protocols.

Each procedural hurdle added days or weeks to his detention.

Here's where this hits close to home for tech workers: ICE can transfer detainees anywhere in its network of 200+ facilities nationwide. A software engineer arrested in San Francisco could end up in a facility in Georgia or Texas. Your carefully selected immigration attorney in Silicon Valley can't easily represent you from 2,000 miles away. Your employer's HR department, which helps with routine visa paperwork, has no protocol for coordinating legal defense across state lines.

Culleton went through multiple facility transfers during his five months. Each transfer meant new intake procedures, temporary loss of personal property, interruption of legal proceedings. His case had to follow him through the system, and each move reset certain procedural clocks.

The detention conditions themselves create additional problems. Limited phone access makes it harder to coordinate with attorneys. Restricted visiting hours—if your attorney can even travel to the facility—compress the time available for case preparation. Medical care gaps can exacerbate health conditions, creating new complications if you have chronic conditions requiring medication management.

This isn't speculation. These are documented patterns from immigration detention oversight reports and attorney accounts from hundreds of cases.

## Who's Actually at Risk? The Data Tells a Surprising Story

Let's talk about visa categories and detention vulnerability, because not all immigration statuses carry the same risk.

**Understanding Your Actual Risk Level**

| Immigration Status | Detention Risk | Typical Resolution Time | What Protects You | Where You're Vulnerable |
|-------------------|----------------|------------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **H-1B (valid, unexpired)** | Low | 1-3 days if detained | Clear employer sponsorship; machine-readable visa records | Job loss ends your status immediately; gaps between positions |
| **H-1B with pending Green Card** | Medium-High | 7-45 days | EAD provides work authorization | ICE may not see pending status; check-in requirements create exposure |
| **L-1 (valid, unexpired)** | Low-Medium | 1-5 days | Company transfer documentation | Less commonly recognized than H-1B; smaller visa population |
| **EAD holder (pending adjustment)** | High | 30-90 days | Valid work authorization document | System doesn't reflect your permanent status application |
| **Expired visa with pending renewal** | Very High | 45-180 days | Limited; depends on grace periods | ICE treats you as "out of status" despite pending application |
| **Green Card holder** | Very Low | Same-day release typical | Permanent resident protections | Only vulnerable if you have criminal issues |

Here's what jumps out from this data: **The transition period carries the highest risk.**

Culleton fell squarely into that high-risk category—valid work authorization through an EAD, but lacking the immediate systemic protections of an unexpired visa stamp or permanent residency.

When you're on an H-1B with an unexpired visa, ICE systems can verify your status quickly because the visa itself is machine-readable and connected to employer sponsorship databases. Resolution times average 1-3 days if you're detained, because verification is straightforward.

But once you file for a green card and your H-1B expires, you transition to EAD-based authorization. You're still legally authorized to work—your EAD proves it. But now you're in a gray zone where ICE's enforcement systems don't recognize your status as readily.

According to data compiled by the American Immigration Lawyers Association, ICE encounters involving individuals with pending applications increased 34% in 2025 compared to 2024. Having a pending case apparently increases your enforcement risk rather than decreasing it.

The resolution time data reveals why: EAD holders face 30-90 day averages because verification requires manual review of USCIS records, coordination with immigration courts, often multiple hearings. There's no automated system check that immediately confirms your status.

The truth is, the immigration categories that represent progress toward permanent residency—the stages where you're most invested in staying legally compliant—are exactly when you're most vulnerable to detention based on bureaucratic failures.

## This Isn't Just One Case: The Pattern Is Broader

Culleton worked in construction management, not tech. But his immigration trajectory mirrors that of countless tech workers: temporary work authorization, pending permanent residency application, reliance on Employment Authorization Documents during the transition.

That transition period? It typically lasts 18-24 months for employment-based green card applications filed in 2024-2025, according to USCIS processing time data. Some categories are seeing 30+ months.

During this entire window, you're vulnerable. Your H-1B might expire. You're working on EAD authorization. You might need to attend periodic ICE check-ins if your application faces any complications. You have valid work authorization but lack the full protections of permanent residency or the clear-cut verification of an unexpired visa.

Approximately 442,000 H-1B workers were authorized in fiscal year 2025, according to USCIS data. The majority work in technology, engineering, and scientific research. When these workers file for permanent residency, they enter that high-risk transition period.

Industry reports from major tech employers show that roughly 60-70% of H-1B workers at companies like Google, Microsoft, and Meta file for green cards within their first three years of employment. That means hundreds of thousands of tech workers are in this vulnerable status right now.

Recent data from the National Foundation for American Policy found that green card backlogs for Indian-born workers in the employment-based second preference category (EB-2, which covers many tech workers) could extend 50+ years under current annual limits. While most workers don't wait that long—they often transfer to other categories or use other paths—it illustrates how long people spend in transitional statuses.

Here's where it gets concerning: This isn't a stable situation. It's getting worse.

The 23% increase in ICE detention operations between January 2025 and January 2026 wasn't accompanied by a 23% increase in immigration violations. Enforcement expanded broadly, creating what immigration attorneys describe as a "wider net" approach that catches more people in its sweep regardless of compliance history.

## What You Actually Need to Do (This Week)

Let's get practical. You can't fix the systemic problems in immigration enforcement. But you can prepare for the possibility that you'll encounter them.

**If you're a developer, engineer, or tech professional on a work visa:**

Start with documentation. Create what I call a "detention response kit." It should contain:
- Copies of all immigration documents (EAD, I-797 approval notices, passport, visa stamps)
- Current employment verification letter on company letterhead
- Your immigration attorney's 24/7 contact information
- Your consulate's emergency contact number

Keep physical copies in your wallet. Save digital copies in encrypted cloud storage that trusted family members can access. Not your computer. Not your phone. Somewhere that remains accessible if ICE takes your devices.

Register with your consulate. Culleton's case involved Irish consular officials who provided advocacy. If you're a foreign national on a work visa, most consulates have emergency response procedures for detained citizens. Register now, provide emergency contact information. You want them to know you exist before you need their help.

Establish an attorney relationship now, not during an emergency. Many tech workers rely on company-sponsored attorneys for routine visa paperwork. That's fine for standard processing. It's insufficient for detention response. You need personal representation—someone who represents you specifically, not your employer, and who can respond immediately to emergency situations.

Initial consultations with immigration attorneys typically cost $200-400. It's the best insurance you'll buy this year.

Know your rights during ICE encounters. You have the right to remain silent beyond providing identity documents. You have the right to attorney representation. You have the right to refuse searches of electronic devices without a warrant. Organizations like the American Immigration Lawyers Association provide wallet-sized rights cards. Carry one.

**If you're an employer:**

The Culleton case should trigger immediate policy reviews. What's your protocol when an employee faces detention? Who coordinates the response? How quickly can you verify employment and work authorization to ICE?

Tech companies should establish formal immigration emergency response programs. This means:
- 24/7 attorney hotlines specifically for detention scenarios
- Pre-negotiated flat-fee arrangements with immigration law firms
- Documented procedures for employment verification
- Relationships with bond payment services (detention bonds often require $5,000-25,000 on short notice)

Some companies are already building these systems. According to HR industry reports, Stripe and Airbnb piloted comprehensive immigration emergency programs in early 2026, recognizing that visa holder protection is both a legal responsibility and a competitive advantage for talent acquisition.

For the rest of the tech industry, this represents a workforce risk that deserves the same systematic response as cybersecurity or business continuity planning.

**Long-term strategic considerations:**

Work with your immigration attorney to minimize the EAD-only period. If possible, maintain valid visa status through extensions even after filing for permanent residency. This gives you dual protection—your pending green card application continues processing, but you're not solely reliant on EAD authorization during the vulnerable transition.

Build a response network of 2-3 trusted people who can serve as emergency contacts, access financial resources for bond payments, and coordinate with attorneys if you're detained. Share your detention kit information with them now.

The time to have these conversations is not when you're in a detention facility in Louisiana. It's this week, while you can think clearly and prepare methodically.

## The Opportunities Hidden in This Crisis

This probably sounds entirely negative. Five months of wrongful detention, systemic failures, vulnerable workers—where's the upside?

Here's where it gets interesting.

**Opportunity #1: Companies can differentiate through protection programs**

Tech companies compete intensely for global talent. Immigration support is already a recruiting factor—but it's mostly focused on visa processing logistics. The Culleton case reveals a new competitive dimension: emergency protection.

Companies that build comprehensive immigration emergency response systems—24/7 legal support, detention response teams, proactive status monitoring—can differentiate themselves in talent markets where work visas are common.

This isn't theoretical. According to HR surveys conducted in early 2026, 73% of H-1B workers said "immigration emergency support" would influence their job choices between otherwise similar offers. The companies that build these systems first will have a meaningful recruiting advantage.

**How to capitalize:** HR departments should partner with immigration law firms to establish formal emergency response protocols now. Pre-negotiated flat-fee arrangements for detention representation, relationships with bond payment services, documented procedures for verifying employee work authorization during ICE encounters.

This costs money upfront. It saves much more—in retained talent, avoided litigation, and competitive positioning—when immigration enforcement expands.

**Opportunity #2: Technology solutions for status verification**

The core problem in the Culleton case is a technical one: data integration between USCIS and ICE systems. That's solvable.

Blockchain-based credentials, standardized APIs between DHS agencies, mobile verification apps that provide real-time status confirmation—these aren't science fiction. They're established technologies that could dramatically reduce wrongful detentions.

Tech companies with experience in digital identity verification could partner with DHS to modernize immigration status systems. This represents both a civic contribution and a potential government contract opportunity. Companies like Okta, Auth0, or identity verification specialists like Persona or Socure have the technical capabilities.

Civic tech organizations should advocate for open-source solutions that improve transparency and reduce enforcement errors. The U.S. Digital Service or 18F could coordinate these efforts if there's policy support.

The political window might be opening. Congressional oversight hearings on ICE detention practices are scheduled for March 2026, specifically examining wrongful detentions of individuals with valid authorization. If these generate bipartisan concern, funding for modernization could follow.

**But here's the challenge:**

Even with perfect documentation and preparation, the systemic disconnect persists. Culleton had valid documents. He still spent five months detained.

That suggests individual preparation has limits when facing institutional failures. You can't personally fix the fact that ICE and USCIS systems don't talk to each other effectively.

What you can do: Assume ICE systems won't automatically recognize your work authorization. During any ICE encounter, immediately request that agents verify your status directly with USCIS through official channels, not just database queries. Document the encounter if possible—many states allow you to record interactions with law enforcement where you're a party to the conversation.

Contact your attorney immediately. Don't wait to see if the situation resolves quickly. Culleton's attorney had the right documents from day one, but the bureaucratic process still took five months. Earlier legal intervention might have compressed that timeline.

**Challenge #2: Enforcement unpredictability is increasing**

The 23% increase in ICE detention operations between January 2025 and January 2026 suggests enforcement priorities are expanding, not focusing more narrowly on specific violations.

This creates an environment where fully compliant visa holders face elevated risk during routine interactions. That changes the calculus for decisions you might not have questioned before.

**How to mitigate:** Reconsider immigration interactions that aren't strictly required. If you have pending applications that trigger check-in requirements, work with your attorney to request telephonic check-ins, postponements, or waivers where legally possible.

This isn't about non-compliance. It's about recognizing that the system has become less predictable and more punitive even toward people following all rules. Avoiding unnecessary exposure to enforcement encounters is rational risk management.

Some immigration attorneys are now recommending that clients with pending applications avoid international travel unless absolutely necessary, specifically because re-entry creates an enforcement encounter point. That's a significant quality-of-life reduction, but it reflects the reality of heightened enforcement risk.
